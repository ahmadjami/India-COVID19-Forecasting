Here's how to think about it: your manager is the head of DS, so don't bring him *how* questions you can answer yourself (which library, how to code a z-score). Bring him **decisions that need his judgment, business context you can't see, and risks he'd want to know about.** That's what makes a junior look thoughtful rather than stuck.

I'll organize this end-to-end, in the order a good conversation would flow. Under each, the questions worth asking and *why they matter* — so you understand them, not just recite them.

## 1. Business framing & success
The thing you most lack as a finance newcomer is *what this signal is actually for*. Ask:
- **"Who consumes this prediction, and what decision do they make with it?"** A return forecast feeding a human trader vs. an automated execution algo are different products. It changes your horizon priorities and latency needs.
- **"What does 'good enough to be useful' look like?"** In finance a model can be 'right' only 52% of the time and still be valuable. Ask what information coefficient / hit-rate / Sharpe would make this worth deploying. Without this you can't tell success from noise.
- **"Is the goal alpha (predicting direction to profit) or execution quality (timing/cost reduction)?"** You're on an execution desk, so likely the latter — confirm it, because it reframes the whole target.

## 2. The target (you've decided log return — pressure-test it)
You've chosen regression on log returns, 3 horizons. Good. But surface these:
- **"Should the target be mid, or microprice?"** You have bid/ask sizes; microprice is usually better short-horizon. Worth his view since it's a modeling-philosophy call.
- **"Do we normalize the return (by volatility / spread), or predict raw log return?"** This is the symbol-agnostic question. A raw +5bps means different things on different names. Frame it as: *"I'm planning to predict vol-normalized log return so it's comparable across instruments — does that fit how the desk would use it?"* You're showing a plan and inviting correction, not asking permission.
- **"Are 5/30/60 min the right horizons, or driven by something specific?"** Maybe the desk only cares about one. Three models is three times the work — confirm all three are wanted.

## 3. Data scope & reality (the questions that save you weeks)
This is where a newcomer earns trust by catching things early:
- **"How much history will we get? Right now I have ~1 day."** *This is the single most important question.* One day can't validate anything (you can't test across market regimes, can't compute causal cross-day statistics). Say plainly: *"I can build the framework on one day, but I won't be able to trust any performance number until we have multiple days/months. What's the path to more data?"*
- **"How were the trades pre-filtered?"** You learned `cond` = continuous flag and the data's already filtered to continuous. Confirm: *"Is anything else stripped (odd lots, off-book, certain venues)?"* — affects what your model has and hasn't seen.
- **"For snapshot markets (India 1s, China 3s), how should I treat quotes between snapshots?"** This is a real open decision in your appendix. He'll have an opinion.
- **"Will the live/production data look exactly like this?"** If you train on clean historical data but production has latency or different fields, the model breaks. Ask now.

## 4. Approach & methodology (show your plan, invite steering)
Don't ask open "what should I do" — present and ask for steering:
- **"My plan is volume bars first (robust to the conflation differences across markets), then dollar bars as an upgrade. Reasonable?"** Shows you understood the data quirk.
- **"For validation I'm using walk-forward with a purge+embargo sized to each horizon, to avoid look-ahead leakage. Is that the standard here?"** Leakage is *the* cardinal sin in trading models — flagging that you're guarding against it signals maturity.
- **"One pooled model across all markets/symbols with dimensionless features, no instrument identity — or does the desk expect per-market models?"** A genuine architecture fork he should weigh in on.

## 5. Evaluation & what 'done' means
- **"What's the baseline I'm being compared against?"** Is there an existing signal? Beating 'predict zero' is the minimum; beating an incumbent model is the real bar.
- **"Should I optimize for ranking (IC), direction (hit-rate), or calibrated magnitude?"** Different metrics, different models. For execution, ranking/direction usually wins.
- **"How will we know it works *out-of-sample* before risking anything?"** Ties back to the data-history question.

## 6. Risks & constraints (managers love a junior who thinks about these)
- **"What's the latency budget?"** If predictions must be live in milliseconds, that constrains feature complexity.
- **"Any compliance/model-governance steps I should design for from the start?"** Banks (HSBC especially) have model-risk processes — knowing early avoids rework.
- **"What's the timeline and what does the v0 milestone need to show?"** So you scope correctly.

---

**Two pieces of framing advice for the conversation itself:**

First, **lead with the data-history question.** It's the one with the biggest impact on everything and the one you can't solve alone. If you only ask one thing, ask that.

Second, **present decisions as proposals, not questions.** Compare *"What target should I use?"* (sounds lost) vs. *"I'm planning vol-normalized log microprice return across three horizons, validated walk-forward with leakage guards — I'd like your steer on the target normalization and whether all three horizons are needed."* The second tells him you've done the thinking and need his judgment on the parts only he can answer. That's exactly the junior↔head-of-DS dynamic that builds trust.
